Chapter 19

Why Do We Need to Correct Errors?

Even experienced accountants make mistakes. The key skill is knowing how to find them and fix them — without making things worse.

When a bookkeeper records a transaction, they follow the double-entry rule: every debit must have a matching credit. But sometimes things go wrong — a number gets transposed, an entry lands in the wrong account, or a transaction is missed entirely.


These errors fall into two broad camps, and understanding the difference is the single most important concept in this chapter:

The Golden Question

Does this error cause the Trial Balance to disagree? If total debits still equal total credits, the TB agrees and the error is "hidden." If they don't match, the TB flags something is wrong. Your approach to correction depends entirely on which camp the error falls into.

In this guide we focus on errors that do NOT affect Trial Balance agreement — the sneaky ones. These are harder to detect because the TB still balances perfectly, yet the books are wrong.

🔑 The Correction Process — Always 3 Steps

1

Identify what was DONE (the wrong entry)

Write out exactly what the bookkeeper recorded. Which accounts were debited and credited, and for how much?

2

Identify what SHOULD have been done (the correct entry)

Write out the proper double entry for the transaction as it should have been recorded.

3

Write the CORRECTING journal entry

This single entry bridges the gap — it reverses the wrong parts and puts in the right parts. Compare steps 1 and 2 to figure out what needs to change.

Classification

The 6 Types of Errors

All six types below do NOT affect trial balance agreement. The TB still balances — but the books are still wrong.

✅ TB Still Agrees

These errors keep debits = credits, so the TB won't flag them.

  • 1. Commission — Wrong account, same type
  • 2. Principle — Wrong account, different type
  • 3. Omission — Entry completely missed
  • 4. Original Entry — Wrong amount, right accounts
  • 5. Complete Reversal — Right accounts, wrong sides
  • 6. Compensating — Multiple errors cancel out

❌ TB Disagrees

These errors break the debit = credit equality.

  • Casting errors in journals
  • One-sided entries (only Dr or only Cr recorded)
  • Posting to wrong side of one account
  • Extraction errors when preparing TB

These go to a Suspense Account — covered separately.

🧠 Memory Trick

Think: "COOPCR" — Commission, Omission, Original entry, Principle, Compensating, Reversal. All six keep the TB balanced because both sides of the double entry are equally affected (or equally unaffected).

Error TypeWhat Went WrongWhy TB Still Agrees
CommissionWrong person/account, same typeSame side, same amount — totals unchanged
PrincipleWrong type of account entirelyDebit is still a debit — total debits unchanged
OmissionNothing recorded at allZero on both sides — balance untouched
Original EntryWrong amount on both sidesBoth Dr and Cr have the same (wrong) amount
ReversalDr ↔ Cr swappedTotals still add up (just on wrong sides)
CompensatingTwo+ errors that offsetNet effect on TB totals is zero
1

Error of Commission

Definition

An entry has been made in a wrong account of the same type. The correct side (Dr or Cr) was used, the amount is correct — but it went to the wrong person or account within the same category.

🔍 How to spot it

The question will say something like "was credited to [Person A] instead of [Person B]" — and both are the same type (e.g., both trade debtors, both trade creditors, both bank accounts).

Worked Example

A trade debtor, Anson, paid the firm $500 by cheque on 18 May 2022, but the accountant had wrongly credited the account of another trade debtor, Adrian.

1

What was DONE (wrong entry)

Dr Bank $500   Asset ↑
Cr Adrian $500   Asset ↓ ← Wrong person!

2

What SHOULD have been done

Dr Bank $500   Asset ↑
Cr Anson $500   Asset ↓ ← Correct person

3

Correcting Entry

The Bank entry is fine — only the credit side is wrong. We need to: remove Adrian (debit him back) and credit Anson instead.

Adrian Trade Receivable Debit to reverse the wrong credit → Adrian's balance goes back up (Asset ↑) 500
Anson Trade Receivable Credit to correctly reduce Anson's balance (Asset ↓) — this is what should have happened 500

2

Error of Principle

Definition

An entry has been made in a wrong type of account. Unlike commission (same type), here the account belongs to a completely different category — e.g., an Asset was recorded as an Expense, or vice versa.

⚠️ Commission vs Principle

Commission = wrong account, same type (e.g., Debtor A → Debtor B).
Principle = wrong account, different type (e.g., Asset → Expense). This one distorts the financial statements!

Worked Example

The firm bought a motor van for $55,000 by cheque on 14 May 2022, but the accountant had wrongly debited the motor expenses account.

1

What was DONE (wrong)

Dr Motor Expenses $55,000   Expense ↑ ← Wrong type!
Cr Bank $55,000   Asset ↓

2

What SHOULD have been done

Dr Motor Vans $55,000   NCA ↑
Cr Bank $55,000   Asset ↓

3

Correcting Entry

Bank is correct. We need to: debit Motor Vans (put the asset in) and credit Motor Expenses (take out the wrong expense).

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Motor Vans NCA Debit to record the asset that was missed → Non-Current Assets ↑ → Balance Sheet 55,000
Motor Expenses Expense Credit to remove the wrongly recorded expense → Expenses ↓ → Net Profit ↑ 55,000
Non-Current Assets
↑ +$55,000 (Motor Vans now recognised)
Expenses
↓ −$55,000 (Motor Expenses reversed)
Net Profit
↑ +$55,000 (less expense = more profit)
Balance Sheet
↑ Assets ↑, Equity ↑ (via retained profit)

💡 Why This Matters

Error of principle is one of the most damaging errors because it affects both the Balance Sheet (assets wrong) and the Income Statement (expenses wrong, so profit is wrong). Commission errors, by contrast, only shuffle amounts within the same category.

Error of Omission

Definition

No entry has been made at all for a transaction. The entire double entry was skipped — both the debit and the credit are missing from the books.

🔍 Why TB still agrees

Since neither side was recorded, both total debits and total credits are equally understated. The trial balance still balances — it just doesn't reflect reality.

Worked Example

The business bought goods on credit from Carson for $2,500 on 12 May 2022. No entries had been made.

1

What was DONE

Nothing. No debit, no credit. The transaction was completely missed.

2

What SHOULD have been done

Dr Purchases $2,500   Expense ↑
Cr Carson $2,500   Trade Payable ↑

3

Correcting Entry

Simply record the transaction as it should have been — the correcting entry IS the correct entry.

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Purchases ExpenseDebit to record the purchase of goods → Expenses ↑ → Net Profit ↓2,500
Carson Trade PayableCredit to record the amount owed to Carson → Liabilities ↑ → Balance Sheet2,500

↑ +$2,500
Trade Payables
↑ +$2,500
Net Profit
↓ −$2,500 (more purchases = less profit)
Balance Sheet
↑ Liabilities ↑, Equity ↓

Error of Original Entry

Definition

An incorrect amount has been entered in the correct accounts on the correct sides. Both debit and credit are wrong by the same amount, so the TB still agrees.

🔍 The Fix is the Difference

Calculate: Correct amount − Wrong amount. If the result is positive, make the same entry for the difference. If negative (overstated), reverse the entry for the difference.

Worked Example

Credit sales of $3,000 to Wilbur was recorded as $3,400 in the books.

1

What was DONE (wrong)

Dr Wilbur $3,400 TR ↑   Cr Sales $3,400 Revenue ↑
Both overstated by $400 ($3,400 − $3,000).

2

What SHOULD have been done

Dr Wilbur $3,000 TR ↑   Cr Sales $3,000 Revenue ↑

3

Correcting Entry

Overstated by $400, so reverse the entry for $400 to bring both accounts down.

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Sales RevenueDebit to reduce overstated sales by $400 → Revenue ↓ → Net Profit ↓400
Wilbur Trade ReceivableCredit to reduce overstated receivable by $400 → Assets ↓400
Trade Receivables
↓ −$400
Sales Revenue
↓ −$400
Net Profit
↓ −$400
Balance Sheet
↓ Assets ↓, Equity ↓

5

Complete Reversal of Entries

Definition

The double entry has been made on the wrong sides of the ledger accounts. The correct accounts and correct amounts were used — but the debit and credit are swapped.

🚨 Critical Rule: Multiply by 2!

The correcting amount is DOUBLE the original amount. Why? You need the original wrong amount reversed plus the correct amount recorded. Think of it as: undo (×1) + redo (×1) = ×2.

Worked Example

The business paid $2,000 cash to a trade creditor, Eric, on 28 May 2022. However, the accountant had made the double entry in reverse.

❌ What was done (wrong)

Dr Cash $2,000 Asset ↑
Cr Eric $2,000 Liability ↑

This shows cash coming IN and owing Eric MORE — exact opposite of reality!

✅ What should have been done

Dr Eric $2,000 Liability ↓
Cr Cash $2,000 Asset ↓

Paying a creditor reduces what you owe (Eric ↓) and reduces cash.

🧮 Why ×2?

Eric's account was credited $2,000 (wrong) but should have been debited $2,000 (correct).
To go from Cr $2,000 → Dr $2,000, you need to debit $4,000 total (first $2,000 cancels the wrong credit, second $2,000 creates the correct debit).
Same logic applies to Cash: Cr $4,000 total needed.

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Eric Trade Payable$2,000 × 2 = $4,000. Debit to reverse wrong Cr ($2,000) AND record correct Dr ($2,000) → Liabilities ↓↓4,000
Cash Asset$2,000 × 2 = $4,000. Credit to reverse wrong Dr ($2,000) AND record correct Cr ($2,000) → Assets ↓↓4,000
Trade Payables (Eric)
↓ −$4,000 net effect
Cash
↓ −$4,000 net effect
Net Profit
→ No effect (BS accounts only)
Balance Sheet
↓ Both Assets and Liabilities ↓

6

Compensating Errors

Definition

Two or more separate errors cancel each other out so that the trial balance still agrees. Each error on its own would cause the TB to disagree — but by pure coincidence, they offset perfectly.

Worked Example

(i) A settlement of $2,000 from a credit customer by cheque was only debited to the bank account. The credit entry to Trade Receivables was missed.
(ii) Cash drawings of $5,000 by the owner was debited to the drawings account as $3,000 (understated by $2,000).

🔍 How they compensate

Error (i): Credit side is missing $2,000 → total credits understated by $2,000.
Error (ii): Debit side is short $2,000 → total debits understated by $2,000.
Both sides are equally understated, so TB still balances!

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Drawings EquityError (ii): Debit the extra $2,000 ($5,000 − $3,000) → Drawings ↑ → Equity ↓2,000
Trade Receivables AssetError (i): Credit to record the missed reduction → Assets ↓2,000
Drawings
↑ +$2,000 (corrected to $5,000)
Trade Receivables
↓ −$2,000 (customer's debt cleared)
Net Profit
→ No effect (neither account hits IS)
Balance Sheet
↓ Assets ↓, Equity ↓

⚠️ Exam Tip

Compensating errors are corrected as separate fixes combined into one journal entry. Treat each error independently, work out what's missing, then combine. Don't try to see them as one error — they're just two unrelated mistakes that happen to offset.

Error in Valuation of Closing Inventory

Scenario

The closing inventory was valued at $20,000. However, it was found that the value was overstated by $4,000 (correct value: $16,000).

💡 Key Concept

Closing inventory appears as: (1) a credit in the Trading Account (reducing COGS), and (2) a debit in the Balance Sheet as a current asset. If it's overstated, COGS is understated → Gross Profit overstated → Net Profit overstated, and Assets are overstated.

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Profit and Loss Expense/LossDebit to reduce profit (overstated COGS correction) → Net Profit ↓4,000

ii

Drawings Recorded as Sales (with Markup)

Scenario

Goods with a selling price of $3,000 were taken by the owner. It had only been recorded as a credit sale. The mark-up was 25% above cost.

🚨 Markup Calculation

Selling Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%)
$3,000 = Cost × 125% → Cost = $3,000 ÷ 1.25 = $2,400
Drawings are always recorded at cost price, not selling price!

1

Step 1: Reverse the wrong sale

Dr Sales $3,000 / Cr Trade Receivables $3,000 — undo the credit sale that never happened

2

Step 2: Record drawings at cost

Dr Drawings $2,400 / Cr Purchases $2,400 — owner took goods at cost value

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Sales RevenueReverse the fake sale → Revenue ↓ → Net Profit ↓3,000
Trade Receivables AssetRemove the fake debtor → Assets ↓3,000
Drawings EquityRecord owner taking goods → Drawings ↑ (at cost $2,400)2,400
Purchases ExpenseReduce purchases (goods not sold, taken by owner) → Expenses ↓ → Net Profit ↑2,400
iii

Prepayment Recorded as Accrual

Scenario

A prepayment of rent of $14,000 was recorded as an accrual.

🚨 Double the Impact!

What was done: Dr Rent $14,000, Cr Accrued Rent $14,000 (expense ↑, liability ↑)
What should have been done: Dr Prepaid Rent $14,000, Cr Rent $14,000 (asset ↑, expense ↓)
Rent expense is wrong by $14,000 + $14,000 = $28,000 because it went the wrong direction!

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Accrued Rent LiabilityRemove the wrong accrual → Liabilities ↓14,000
Prepaid Rent AssetCreate the correct prepayment → Assets ↑14,000
Rent Expense$14,000 + $14,000 = $28,000 reduction → Expenses ↓ → Net Profit ↑ $28,00028,000
iv

Error in Recording in Books of Original Entry

Scenario

The business sold goods on credit to Daniel for $1,500 on 13 May 2022. However, $1,300 was recorded in the sales journal.

💡 How Sales Journal Works

Individual entries in the sales journal are posted one-by-one to each debtor's account. The total is posted to the Sales account at period end. Since the wrong amount ($1,300) was recorded, both Daniel's account and the Sales total are understated by $200.

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Daniel Trade ReceivableDebit the extra $200 ($1,500 − $1,300) → Assets ↑200
Sales RevenueCredit the extra $200 → Revenue ↑ → Net Profit ↑200

⚠️ Casting vs Recording Error

If the error is in casting (adding up the total) of the sales journal, it affects only the Sales account total — NOT individual debtor accounts. This WOULD affect TB agreement because only one side changes. Here, the individual entry itself was wrong, so both sides are equally affected.

Disposal of NCA Recorded as Sales of Goods

Scenario

On 1 January 2020, cash sales of a motor van for $12,000 had been recorded as cash sales of goods. Policy: 10% p.a. on cost. The motor van had cost $60,000 with accumulated depreciation of $40,000 as at 31 Dec 2019. Year end: 31 Dec 2020.

🚨 Most Complex Scenario

This requires three corrections:
1. Reverse any depreciation wrongly charged for 2020 (van was disposed on 1 Jan, so NO depreciation for 2020).
2. Reverse the wrongly recorded sales entry.
3. Record the proper disposal (remove cost, remove acc. dep., recognise loss/gain).

1

Reverse 2020 Depreciation

The van was sold on 1 Jan 2020 — it shouldn't have been depreciated for 2020.
Wrongly charged: $60,000 × 10% = $6,000

2

Calculate NBV at Disposal

Cost $60,000 − Acc. Dep. $40,000 = NBV $20,000 (at 1 Jan 2020)

3

Calculate Loss on Disposal

Sale proceeds $12,000 − NBV $20,000 = Loss $8,000

📝 Correcting Journal Entry
AccountDr ($)Cr ($)
Accumulated Depreciation: MV Contra NCAReverse 2020's wrong depreciation: $60,000 × 10% = $6,0006,000
Depreciation: MV ExpenseRemove wrong 2020 depreciation charge → Expenses ↓6,000
Sales RevenueReverse the wrongly recorded sale → Revenue ↓12,000
Accumulated Depreciation: MV Contra NCARemove acc. dep. on disposal (bal at 31/12/2019)40,000
Loss on Disposal: MV LossBalancing figure: $60,000 − $12,000 − $40,000 = $8,0008,000
Motor Vans NCARemove the motor van at cost → NCA ↓ $60,00060,000

Total Dr: $6,000 + $12,000 + $40,000 + $8,000 = $66,000
Total Cr: $6,000 + $60,000 = $66,000 ✓

Donation of Goods

The Rule

When a firm donates goods to a charity, the goods leave inventory (reduce purchases), and a donation expense is recognised.

📝 Journal Entry for Goods Donation

Contra Entries

What is a Contra Entry?

When a person is both a trade debtor (they owe you) and a trade creditor (you owe them), you can set off the overlapping amount. This is called a contra entry.

Worked Example

The business had: Michael (trade payables) $7,000 and Michael (trade receivables) $4,000. It decided to set off the amount owed from Michael by a contra entry.

💡 Logic

Michael owes you $4,000, and you owe Michael $7,000. Why not cancel out the overlap? Set off the smaller amount ($4,000). After: you owe Michael $3,000, and Michael owes you $0.
